Shoshin, zanshin, mushin, and fudoshin are four states of mind described in Zen Buddhism. These states of mind help one to approach life with wisdom and with an attitude befitting an aspiration to enlightenment.
12:40‚ÄØAM
Shoshin is "beginner's mind." It is the quality of seeing oneself as forever a student, forever a beginner. Rather than cleaving onto the assumption of expertise, it's an attitude of dropping the ego in favor of keeping an open mind and an enthusiasm for learning. When failures arise, instead of letting it bruise one's pride, they merely offer new challenges to climb over.
12:42‚ÄØAM
Zanshin is "lingering mind" or "remaining mind." It's a state of relaxed awareness and preparation. Alert, vigilant, but not tense, and with a mindset of self-control. It can be helpful to compare it to the way you would hold a small bird: you want to have your fingers closed to not let it fly away, but not so tight you hurt it. It's a careful, gentle kind of grip. Relax your grip, but still hold on. That, I think, is a good way to describe zanshin.
12:43‚ÄØAM
In martial arts, it also refers to the mental state of continuation from the completion of one technique to the initiation of the next. It's the readiness to attack even as the opponent falls, but the restraint to not attack a single time more than necessary.
12:46‚ÄØAM
Mushin is "no-mind." This is a state of emptiness and clarity that is the precondition for enlightenment in Zen Buddhism. It's a state of not being lost in thought, of being attenuated to the senses and the environment, aware and able to respond to anything as it occurs. It isn't always a complete cessation of thought, but rather a state of not clinging to or becoming mired in thought.
12:47‚ÄØAM
Emptiness is often scary to Westerners, but void is not dead or sterile in Buddhist thought or the view point of different Eastern religions. Void is a precondition for life; after all, your lungs couldn't accept air if they were a solid mass. It's the cavities within the body, within the blood vessels, the organs, that allow them to carry out the functions of life. Space is the canvas for movement. Something must be empty before it can be filled.
12:48‚ÄØAM
It's also comparable to flow state or being "in the Zone."
12:50‚ÄØAM
Fudoshin is "immovable mind." It takes its name from Fudo Myo'o, the Japanese name for Acala, the preeminent Wisdom King and defender of the dharma. Acala is relentless and unswerving in his defense of the Buddha's teachings and those who have taken refuge in those teachings. Likewise, fudoshin is the quality of being inexorable, immovable, and inevitable in one's pursuit of the way. The mind may bend, but doesn't break. It may flow around obstacles like a river around the rocks, but it never stops moving towards its goal.
